The Pain Points of Traditional Document Handling\n\n**Volume and Variety** – A typical EPC contract generates 5,000–10,000 pages of documentation, ranging from PDFs and CAD drawings to Excel BOMs and Word specifications.\n\n**Manual Data Extraction** – Engineers often copy‑paste numbers from PDFs into spreadsheets, a process prone to transcription errors and version mismatches.\n\n**Change‑Order Chaos** – When a design change occurs, the associated documents—drawings, specs, cost tables—must be identified, revised, and re‑distributed. Tracking which version is the latest becomes a nightmare.\n\n**Compliance Bottlenecks** – Regulatory requirements demand that every document be indexed with metadata (project code, discipline, revision date). Maintaining this manually adds overhead and risks non‑compliance.\n\nThese challenges create hidden costs estimated at 10‑15% of total project spend. AI automation can shave hours off each task, translating directly into dollars saved.\n\n---\n\n## 2. Real‑World Example: Automating Vendor Data‑Sheet Extraction\n\n**The Scenario** – An EPC contractor receives 200 vendor data sheets per week, each in a different PDF layout. The team must extract key fields (equipment model, capacity, pressure rating, material) and feed them into a central specification database.\n\n**AI Solution** – Using KU Automation’s document‑processing pipeline, we combine OCR (via Tesseract) with a fine‑tuned LLM (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) to:\n\n1. **Detect Table Regions** – Vision model identifies tables within the PDF.\n2. **Extract Text** – OCR converts the table image to raw text.\n3. **Structure Data** – Prompt engineering guides the LLM to map raw rows into a JSON schema: `{ "model": ..., "capacity": ..., "pressure": ..., "material": ... }`.\n4. **Validate** – Simple business rules (e.g., pressure > 0, material ∈ {"Carbon Steel", "Stainless Steel"}) flag anomalies for review.\n\n**Outcome** – What used to take 5 minutes per sheet (100 hours/month) is now processed in under 10 seconds with 95% accuracy. Engineers spend the remaining 5% of cases correcting edge‑cases, freeing up roughly 80 hours of senior engineer time each month.\n\n---\n\n## 3. Real‑World Example: AI‑Powered Change‑Order Summarization\n\n**The Challenge** – A change order generates a 30‑page PDF amendment that includes revised drawings, cost tables, and narrative justifications. Project managers must read the whole document to understand impact.\n\n**AI Workflow** – KU Automation leverages a summarization chain:\n\n1. **Chunking** – The PDF is split into logical sections (Scope, Drawings, Cost Impact).\n2. **Summarize** – Each chunk is passed to an LLM (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) with a prompt: “Summarize the engineering impact of this section in ≤2 sentences.”\n3. **Consolidate** – Summaries are merged into an executive‑level bullet list.\n4. **Highlight Risks** – A second pass extracts any risk keywords ("delay", "budget overrun", "safety").\n\n**Result** – Project leads receive a 5‑bullet summary within seconds, enabling rapid decision‑making and reducing the average change‑order approval time from 3 days to <12 hours.\n\n---\n\n## 4.
